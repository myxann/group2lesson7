Women and Public Life

The Grant of Suffrage to Filipino Women

Until the mid-1930s, only men in the Philippines had the right to vote. The 1935 Constitution initially restricted suffrage to male citizens aged 21 and above. However, it included a provision allowing women to vote if at least 300,000 qualified women approved it in a plebiscite.

This led to Commonwealth Act No. 34, which facilitated a plebiscite on April 30, 1937. Nearly 500,000 women voted in favor, granting Filipino women the right to vote and run for office. Since then, women have played an active role in Philippine politics, including the election of two female presidents: Corazon Aquino (1986-1992) and Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo (2001-2010).

The 1973 and 1987 Constitutions further expanded voting rights by lowering the voting age from 21 to 18 years old. Today, Filipino women hold key positions across the government, including in the Senate, House of Representatives, Judiciary, and national commissions.

This historic milestone remains one of the most significant achievements of the Philippine Commonwealth Government (1935-1946) in advancing women's rights and political participation.

First Woman President of the Republic

Corazon "Cory" Aquino made history as the first woman President of the Philippines and a global icon of democracy. Rising to power after the People Power Revolution in 1986, she ended decades of dictatorship and restored democratic governance in the country.

Cory Aquino’s presidency was more than a political shift—it was a defining moment for women empowerment in politics. Breaking barriers in a male-dominated arena, she led the nation with strength, integrity, and compassion, proving that women could hold the highest office in the land.

Her leadership saw the creation of the 1987 Constitution, reinforcing democracy and securing civil liberties, including greater political participation for women. This paved the way for more women in government, highlighting their vital role in shaping the nation’s future.

However, this also poses several challenges to the women in political environment such as limited family and societal support, that leads to fewer women than men run for public office, as well a woman running for politics are less encouraged unlike their male counterparts.

Today, Aquino’s legacy stands as a testament to the power of women in leadership. Her presidency remains a milestone in the fight for gender equality, inspiring generations to take an active role in governance—not just as voters, but as leaders driving meaningful change.

Women in Politics

Women's participation in politics promotes developmental changes on the political processes and value systems. Female politicians can help lessen corruption and abuse in government, as well as boldly addressing a nation's problem and utilizing the government's power to address concerns such as crime reduction, ecological conservation, and improvement of education and social welfare policies.

Proportion of seats held by women in national parliaments in the Philippines (2014 to 2023)

The 27.33% proportion of women in the Philippine national parliament in 2023 remained unchanged from the previous year. This statistic highlights women's participation in lawmaking but also underscores that gender parity in political leadership has yet to be achieved.

Rise of Elected Filipino Women (1998 - 2016)

Source: https://www.esquiremag.ph/politics/news/women-in-philippine-government-a00287-20190313?s=iu2g51rke54fn4vn82t9l47qpe

The trend of increasing female participation in politics is evident in the Philippines' public sector. Over the years, the number of elected women has consistently grown, except for a slight 2.6% decline in 2004, as reported by the Commission on Elections (Comelec). By 2016, women held 21.5% of all elected positions—the highest percentage recorded in the past seven election cycles.

Additionally, the 2016 elections saw the highest proportion of female candidates at 19.4%, demonstrating a growing willingness among women to run for public office. This figure includes candidates across all elective roles in the executive and legislative branches, as well as those in local government units.

Despite this progress, women still make up a minority in the political landscape.

The Asean Parliamentarians for Human Rights (APHR) revealed on International Women's Day that women's representation in Southeast Asia's parliaments is "very low." Among the countries, Timor-Leste has the highest proportion at 33.8%, followed by the Philippines at slightly over 30%, while Thailand has the lowest at just 5.3%.

1 out of 5 Elected Officials are Women

According to Philippine Commission on Women (2018), ONLY ONE in five elected officials in the Philippines is a woman; while a 2.13% increase of female elected council members in Barangays; and 1.14% increase for the position of women village chief or punong barangay.
